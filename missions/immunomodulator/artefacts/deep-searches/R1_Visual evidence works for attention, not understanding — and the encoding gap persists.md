# Visual evidence works for attention, not understanding — and the encoding gap persists

**Graphical abstracts reliably multiply social media engagement by 2–8x, yet no controlled study has shown they improve clinician comprehension or clinical decision-making.** This asymmetry is the central finding of this compilation: the field has robust evidence for *attention capture* but a near-vacuum for *knowledge transfer*. Meanwhile, the semiotics of evidence strength — how to visually encode that 18 RCTs outweigh preclinical data — remains an unsolved design problem. No existing tool parametrically maps evidence certainty to visual properties, despite decades of research on uncertainty visualization, perceptual hierarchies, and risk communication that provide a clear blueprint for doing so. This report synthesizes quantitative evidence across both axes to inform the SciSense methodology.

## AXIS A: MEASURED EFFECTIVENESS OF VISUAL REPRESENTATIONS IN SCIENCE

### Every randomized study shows engagement gains; every observational study does not

The evidence base on graphical abstract (GA) and visual abstract (VA) effectiveness divides cleanly along methodological lines. **All six randomized or prospective crossover studies** report statistically significant improvements in social media engagement metrics. **Both retrospective observational studies** report null or negative effects. This pattern is not contradictory — it reveals that context of dissemination, not the visual format alone, drives impact.

Ibrahim et al. (2017, *Annals of Surgery*) established the field with a prospective crossover of 44 articles, finding that tweets with visual abstracts generated **7.7× more impressions**, **8.4× more retweets**, and **2.7× more article visits** than text-only tweets. Oska et al. (2020, *JMIR*) strengthened the case with a triple crossover design (n=40 articles) that added a critical third arm — tweets with key figures from the article — and found VAs still outperformed both text-only (5× engagements) and key-figure tweets (3.5× engagements), establishing that the *structured summary format*, not merely any image, drives the effect.

The largest study to date is Trueger et al. (2023, *JAMA*), a random-sequence simultaneous crossover across **12 JAMA Network journals and 205 RCTs**. It introduced a nuanced finding: *how* VAs are displayed matters as much as whether they are present. Linking VAs (where clicking the image opens the article) generated significantly more link clicks than expandable VAs or article figures (median 18 vs. 11 vs. 9; P<.001), but this effect was platform-specific — significant on Twitter, nonsignificant on Facebook. Chapman et al. (2019, *British Journal of Surgery*) contributed the first true three-arm RCT (n=41), showing VAs primarily boosted **healthcare professional engagement** (mean 45.3 vs. 28.8 for standard tweets) rather than public engagement. Hoffberg et al. (2020, *Frontiers*) confirmed the pattern in suicide prevention research (n=50, P<.001 for impressions and retweets), while Chisari et al. (2021, *Journal of Arthroplasty*) found an effect size of **d=1.68** (P=.016) for composite engagement in orthopedic surgery, though with only 20 articles.

Against these, Pferschy-Wenzig et al. (2016, *Molecules*) analyzed 1,326 articles and found those *without* GAs had significantly higher PDF downloads, abstract views, and citations. Aggarwal (2021, *Health Information & Libraries Journal*) examined 307 articles across NEJM, BMJ, and JAMA and found no significant differences in Altmetric scores (P=.37), page views (P=.44), or citations. Both studies are retrospective and observational, meaning self-selection bias likely confounds results — authors who chose not to create GAs may have written higher-impact papers, and articles in top-tier journals may face ceiling effects on visibility. Critically, neither study involved active social media dissemination; both examined passive hosting on journal websites.

Vorland et al. (2024, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology*) adds an important cautionary finding: a cross-sectional evaluation of 253 RCT visual abstracts found **inadequate reporting completeness and high prevalence of spin** compared to written abstracts. VAs may boost reach while sacrificing accuracy — a tradeoff any systematic methodology must address.

### The comprehension-engagement gap is real and consistent

The most consequential finding for SciSense is the persistent dissociation between engagement metrics and actual comprehension. Bredbenner and Simon (2019, *PLOS ONE*) directly tested this with 538 participants randomized to view video abstracts, graphical abstracts, plain language summaries, or published abstracts for two Nature papers. **Graphical abstracts ranked third of four formats** for both perceived understanding and measured comprehension, behind video abstracts and plain language summaries. Most critically, the authors found that "reported preference for different summary types was **generally not correlated to comprehension**" — a direct preference-performance gap.

This dissociation extends beyond GAs. Chen et al. (2025, *MDPI Informatics*) found in a within-subjects experiment (N=121) that participants preferred hybrid icon array layouts that actually **impaired their comprehension** compared to the format that maximized understanding (P<.001). Egan et al. (2021, *BMC Public Health*) tested five COVID-19 public health visual formats in a large RCT (N=4,099) and found infographics produced significantly higher **recall scores** than text-only guidance (7.34–7.40 vs. 6.97, P<.001), with the Behavioural Insights Team infographic producing the highest confidence (87%). This is one of the few studies measuring actual knowledge retention rather than engagement.

The systematic review by Garcia-Retamero and Cokely (2017, *Human Factors*) provides the strongest evidence that well-designed visual aids *can* improve genuine understanding. Across 36 publications and **27,885 participants from 60 countries**, "transparent visual aids robustly improved risk understanding in diverse individuals." The key qualifier is "transparent" — the visual must make the underlying data relationships clear, not merely attract attention. Icon arrays were especially effective for individuals with low numeracy, a finding replicated across multiple populations.

### Altmetrics measure attention, not impact — and the two barely correlate

Bennett and Slattery (2023, *Scientometrics*) quantified the engagement-impact disconnect: across 562 sport science articles, those with graphical abstracts had significantly higher Altmetric attention scores (**IRR 1.89, 95% CI 1.25–2.85, P=.003**) but showed **no difference in citations** (IRR 0.97, P=.829). Zong, Huang, and Deng (2023, *Learned Publishing*) used propensity score matching on *Molecules* articles and found GAs increased abstract page clicks but produced "no advantage in receiving more usage of full text and citations." The GA functions as a top-of-funnel attractor that does not convert to deeper scholarly engagement.

Meta-analytic evidence confirms the weak altmetrics-citations relationship. Erdt et al. (2016, *Scientometrics*) analyzed 40+ cross-metric validation studies and found overall correlations of **r=0.08 to 0.50** between altmetrics and citation counts, concluding altmetrics measure "a different kind of research impact." A 2025 *Frontiers* systematic review found Twitter activity correlates strongly with Altmetric scores (r=0.65) but weakly with citations (r=0.16). Only Mendeley readership shows a strong citation correlation (R=0.889–0.905). For SciSense, this means **engagement metrics cannot serve as proxies for comprehension or scholarly impact** — the methodology must be validated with direct comprehension measurement.

### Clinical decision-making evidence favors visual aids but not graphical abstracts specifically

No study was identified that directly tests whether graphical abstracts change clinical decisions or prescribing behavior. The strongest evidence for visual formats affecting clinical decisions comes from risk communication research. Garcia-Retamero and Cokely (2013, *Current Directions in Psychological Science*) reviewed evidence showing visual aids are "often highly effective, transparent, and ethically desirable tools for improving decision making, changing attitudes, and reducing risky behavior" across diverse vulnerable populations. In one included study, **292 surgeons from 60 countries** who received visual risk representations showed significantly better risk estimation than those receiving numbers alone. Zikmund-Fisher et al. (2014, *Medical Decision Making*, N=1,504) found icon type significantly affected risk perceptions and recall in cardiovascular risk communication — person-type icons increased perceived risk compared to blocks or ovals.

## AXIS B: THE SEMIOTICS OF EVIDENCE — HOW TO VISUALLY ENCODE LEVEL OF PROOF

### GRADE's four circles are the only evidence-tested visual encoding of certainty

The GRADE framework's filled-circle system (⊕⊕⊕⊕ for High through ⊕⊖⊖⊖ for Very Low) represents the most widely adopted and empirically tested visual encoding of evidence certainty. Akl et al. (2007, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology*) demonstrated its superiority in an RCT of 84 participants: **symbols produced 74% correct understanding** of recommendation strength versus **14% for numbers** (P<.001). Symbols also scored higher for ease of understanding (mean difference=1.5, P=.001) and clarity/conciseness (md=1.5, P<.001). Approximately 48% of participants preferred symbols over numbers.

The GRADE system's design traces to Schünemann et al. (2003, *CMAJ*), who consulted psychologists, marketing experts, and graphic designers and examined consumer rating systems (Michelin, Zagat, Consumer Reports). They found **no health care research existed** addressing optimal visual representation of evidence grades — a gap that, remarkably, persists over 20 years later for generalized parametric encoding. The four-level system follows Miller's (1956) capacity limit; symbols were chosen for cross-linguistic comprehension and scan-ability.

Cochrane Summary of Findings (SoF) tables embed the GRADE symbols within a standardized six-column format. Rosenbaum et al. (2010, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology*) tested these in two RCTs: in the more rigorous trial (n=33), participants using SoF tables achieved **93% correct comprehension** versus **44% without** (P=.003) and found answers faster. Carrasco-Labra et al. (2016, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology*) showed that modified formats with risk differences and narrative statements improved understanding by **5–47%** depending on the information element (n=290). Morgan et al. (2023, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology*) found that adding Number Needed to Treat and narrative summaries improved user satisfaction, recommending tailoring table content to end-user type.

Klugar et al. (2022, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology*) analyzed eight international guidelines and found **significant heterogeneity in visual presentation** of recommendations, proposing unification into GRADE symbols. Their conclusion is direct: "We discourage the use of letters and numbers as it creates confusion with many other classification systems, and to use only the GRADE symbols."

### The evidence pyramid is intuitive but dangerously oversimplified

The traditional evidence pyramid — basic science at the base, systematic reviews at the apex — succeeds as a spatial metaphor because vertical position pre-attentively communicates hierarchy. But Murad et al. (2016, *Evidence-Based Medicine*) argued it is too simplistic. They proposed two modifications: **wavy lines** between study design levels (reflecting that a well-executed observational study can outrank a poorly done RCT) and repositioning systematic reviews as a **"lens"** through which evidence is viewed, not a study design type. No direct usability studies testing the pyramid's effect on understanding were identified, suggesting its ubiquity rests on intuitive appeal rather than evidence.

The Oxford Centre for Evidence-Based Medicine (CEBM) Levels of Evidence (2011 revision) uses a purely tabular format organized by clinical question type and evidence level. It has **no standardized visual encoding** — no symbols, colors, or icons — and no known usability studies. It functions as a search heuristic rather than a communication tool.

### Uncertainty visualization: blur is intuitive but not quantifiable; animation works best

Jessica Hullman's hypothetical outcome plots (HOPs) represent the most empirically validated alternative to static uncertainty displays. Kale et al. (2019, *IEEE TVCG*) found that observers using HOPs **correctly inferred underlying trends at lower evidence thresholds** than users of error bars or static line ensembles, measured through psychometric function modeling. HOPs leverage the visual system's ensemble processing capability — the ability to extract summary statistics from groups of objects within ~200ms. Error bars, by contrast, suffer from systematic "within-the-bar bias" and encode ambiguous constructs.

Lace Padilla's cognitive framework (2018, *Cognitive Research: Principles and Implications*) identified two critical phenomena. First, **deterministic construal error**: viewers consistently interpret uncertainty information as deterministic, a visual-spatial bias that occurs early in encoding and persists even with instruction. Second, **visual boundaries create cognitive categories**: hard visual boundaries (like the hurricane cone of uncertainty's 66% confidence line) force categorical thinking on continuous data. Her 2022 COVID-19 forecast visualization study (IEEE VIS Best Paper) confirmed that frequency-based visual techniques reduce these misinterpretations.

MacEachren et al. (2012, *IEEE TVCG*) empirically ranked visual properties for uncertainty encoding by intuitiveness:

- **Fuzziness/blur**: Highest intuitiveness but cannot be quantified — viewers distinguish sharp from blurred but cannot differentiate blur levels (Kosara, 2001). Effective only as a binary signal.
- **Color value/brightness**: High intuitiveness, measurable for graded encoding. Lighter = less certain maps naturally.
- **Size**: Above-average intuitiveness but subject to Stevens' power law compression (β≈0.7 for area), meaning larger differences are perceptually compressed.
- **Transparency/opacity**: Above average but with wide individual variation.
- **Color saturation**: Surprisingly **not judged as intuitive** for uncertainty despite designer assumptions — and ranked near the bottom of Cleveland and McGill's accuracy hierarchy.

Kinkeldey, MacEachren, and Schiewe's (2017) systematic review of uncertainty visualization user studies recommended color hue, color value, and transparency while explicitly **not recommending** color saturation for uncertainty depiction.

### Risk communication: natural frequencies and icon arrays change understanding

Gerd Gigerenzer's work demonstrates that representational format dramatically affects comprehension of probabilistic information. When HIV counselors received conditional probabilities, they produced ~50% correct Bayesian inferences (with nearly all errors being catastrophic). With natural frequencies ("10 out of 1,000"), the correct answer becomes transparent. McDowell and Jacobs (2017, *Psychological Bulletin*) confirmed this in a meta-analysis. Galesic, Garcia-Retamero, and Gigerenzer (2009, *Health Psychology*) found icon arrays increased accuracy for **both low- and high-numeracy individuals** (N=59 older adults, N=112 students), even when transparent numerical formats were also provided.

The Paling Perspective Scale — a logarithmic "Richter Scale for Risks" — contextualizes unfamiliar risks alongside familiar ones. Keller and Siegrist (2009, *Medical Decision Making*, N=266) found a significant three-way interaction: **high-numeracy participants** correctly distinguished risk levels with the scale (P<.001), but **low-numeracy participants showed no differentiation** across any format. Icon arrays (pictographs) produced significantly lower (more accurate) risk ratings than the Paling Scale (P<.001). This suggests logarithmic contextualizing scales work only for numerate audiences.

Forest plots remain the gold standard for meta-analytic evidence but are poorly understood by non-epidemiologist clinicians. Multiple teaching tutorials exist specifically because comprehension remains low. Key challenges include interpreting confidence interval width as precision, understanding the line of no effect, interpreting heterogeneity (I²), and reading weight (box size). Hahn, Dizman, and Msaouel (2022, *Therapeutic Advances in Medical Oncology*) found most subgroup analyses using forest plots at ASCO presentations were inconclusive but frequently misinterpreted.

### Cleveland and McGill's hierarchy provides the encoding backbone

Cleveland and McGill's (1984, *JASA*; 1985, *Science*) perceptual accuracy hierarchy remains the foundational ordering for visual encoding design:

1. **Position along a common scale** — most accurate
2. **Position along nonaligned scales**
3. **Length**
4. **Direction/Angle/Slope**
5. **Area**
6. **Volume**
7. **Color saturation/hue/density** — least accurate

Stewart and Best (2010) replicated the hierarchy but found that in practice, **three or four distinct ranking tiers** may be sufficient rather than the full ten-element ordering. Munzner updated the framework, confirming position and length dominate, with luminance and saturation near the bottom for quantitative data.

The "weighted average illusion" (2021, *IEEE TVCG*) demonstrates a systematic bias: in bubble charts, larger and darker points receive disproportionate cognitive weight in mean estimation (P<.0001 for large size ranges). Stevens' power law quantifies the compression: area perception follows β≈0.7 (systematic underestimation of larger values), while length follows β≈1.0 (nearly unbiased). This means **using circle size to encode "weight of evidence" will systematically compress perceived differences** — a 10× difference in evidence volume would be perceived as roughly 5×.

Pre-attentive visual features (Treisman, 1985; Healey, Booth, and Enns, 1996) are processed in under 200–250ms before conscious attention. For evidence strength encoding, the most actionable features are **color hue** (for categorical evidence type distinctions — up to ~12 discriminable levels), **color luminance** (for graded strength — darker = stronger), and **line length/position** (for quantitative magnitude). Blur functions pre-attentively only as a binary feature. An important asymmetry exists: adding a distinctive mark to signal strength (e.g., a bold icon for strong evidence) is more effective than removing features to signal weakness.

### No existing tool parametrically maps evidence to visual properties

This is the most significant gap identified. Despite GRADE, GRADEpro, iSoF (Cochrane's interactive Summary of Findings), MAGICapp, and evidence gap mapping tools (EPPI-Mapper, 3ie, EviAtlas), **no tool systematically translates evidence certainty levels into a parameterized visual design system** — e.g., automatically mapping High certainty → full opacity, solid borders, saturated color versus Very Low certainty → reduced opacity, dashed borders, desaturated color.

GRADEpro uses the standard ⊕ symbol system. iSoF (developed through the EU DECIDE project and integrated via Epistemonikos) adds layered progressive disclosure for different audiences but does not extend the visual vocabulary beyond standard SoF tables. MAGICapp (used by WHO, Australian Living Evidence Collaboration, and European Stroke Organisation) emphasizes "multi-layer" formats but relies on the same GRADE symbology. Evidence gap maps use bubble size, color intensity, and spatial position to encode evidence volume and quality, with Fredlund et al. (2024, *Cochrane Evidence Synthesis and Methods*) identifying 11 visualization designs across 145 maps. Emerging AI tools like OpenEvidence (partnered with NEJM and JAMA in 2025) aggregate and present evidence narratively rather than through parametric visual encoding.

## Conclusion: A design specification for encoding evidence strength visually

This compilation reveals three actionable insights for the SciSense methodology. First, the visual abstract literature demonstrates that structured visual summaries reliably capture **attention** (2–8× engagement gains across all experimental studies), but this attention does not translate to comprehension, citations, or clinical behavior change. Any Visual Evidence Compiler must be validated against comprehension and decision-quality outcomes, not engagement proxies.

Second, the perceptual science converges on a clear encoding hierarchy for evidence strength. **Position on a common scale and length** should carry the primary quantitative signal (Cleveland and McGill). **Color luminance** (not saturation) should encode graded certainty. **Discrete, countable representations** — icon arrays, quantile dotplots, or hypothetical outcome plots — outperform continuous encodings for non-statisticians (Hullman, Padilla, Gigerenzer). **Natural frequency framing** ("3 of 5 studies found..." rather than "60%") dramatically improves Bayesian reasoning. Blur should be used only as a binary uncertain/certain flag. Area and size introduce systematic compression bias (Stevens' β≈0.7) and should serve as secondary channels only, always with numerical labels.

Third, and most consequential for SciSense: **the parametric mapping of evidence certainty to visual properties is an open problem**. GRADE's four-circle system is the closest existing solution, and its superiority over numbers is empirically established (74% vs. 14% comprehension). But it uses only a single visual channel (fill level) and has never been tested outside tabular presentation. The field lacks a general-purpose design system that combines position, luminance, discrete frequency, and progressive disclosure into a coherent visual grammar for evidence strength — exactly the gap SciSense aims to fill. The research base compiled here provides the empirical constraints for building it.
